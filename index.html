Why these numbers?

The minimum mode prescriptions come from a small set of well-replicated findings.

≈4 sets/muscle/week is the minimum effective dose for hypertrophy. A single set, 2–3× per week, still produces measurable gains.
Iversen, Norum, Schoenfeld & Fimland (2021). No Time to Lift? Designing Time-Efficient Training Programs for Strength and Hypertrophy: A Narrative Review. Sports Medicine 51, 2079–2095.
As few as 3 working sets per week per lift drives 1RM strength gains in resistance-trained men.
Androulakis-Korakakis, Fisher & Steele (2020). The Minimum Effective Training Dose Required to Increase 1RM Strength in Resistance-Trained Men: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. Sports Medicine 50, 751–765.
The optimum hypertrophy zone is 10–20 sets per muscle per week. Below 4 sets, adaptation becomes uncertain.
Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2017). Dose-response relationship between weekly resistance training volume and increases in muscle mass: A systematic review and meta-analysis. Journal of Sports Sciences 35, 1073–1082.
For time-efficient sessions: prioritize compounds, restrict warm-ups to exercise-specific only, and prioritize proximity-to-failure over total volume.
Iversen et al. (2021), as above. The "No Time to Lift?" framework.
The 22-Minute Health Minimum is a daily substitute for the full AM/PM plan, not a weekly-target shortcut. It preserves the training thread on low-resource days. It does not complete the weekly volume target by itself — it moves the number in the right direction while keeping continuity.
Design rationale, grounded in Iversen et al. (2021) and Schoenfeld et al. (2017). The full plan remains the optimum; the daily minimum is the floor.
Bodyweight 22 is the equipment-free version of the daily minimum. Travel-ready, low-setup. It is not equal to the main hypertrophy plan, but bodyweight sets taken close enough to effort drive a real movement stimulus and preserve the habit. Push-Up, Bodyweight Squat, Reverse Lunge, Glute Bridge, Prone W Raise, Plank — six fixed movements, ~12 working sets, short rest. Pull is intentionally not included; honest bodyweight pulling needs equipment (bar, table, doorway, strap, towel, backpack), and the Prone W Raise covers upper-back / rear-delt / posture work without pretending to replace it.
Continuity, not heroics. Same Iversen et al. (2021) framework — proximity-to-failure on the available movements is what keeps the chain alive.
Recovery counts. Adaptation happens during rest, not during training.
General sports-science consensus. Minimum-mode "Recovery Logged" preserves continuity without working sets.
